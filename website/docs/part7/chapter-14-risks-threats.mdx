# Chapter 14: The Risks and Threats of AI Humanoid Robots

> **Part VII — Cons — Risks and Security Threats**
> *Version 1.0 — Draft | Author: Muhammad Faisal Laiq Siddiqui*

---

## 14.1 Introduction — A Rigorous Risk Taxonomy

The discourse around AI humanoid robots oscillates between two poles: uncritical enthusiasm that treats every deployment announcement as evidence of imminent technological utopia, and catastrophizing that imagines Terminator-class autonomous weapons deployed against humanity. Neither posture serves the analysis that policymakers, engineers, and the public require.

A rigorous treatment of risk requires a taxonomy — a structured classification of hazard types by their nature, their proximity, their evidence base, and their magnitude. This chapter applies such a taxonomy to the risks presented by AI humanoid robots. Six primary risk categories are examined: physical safety, cybersecurity, privacy, economic disruption, psychological and social harm, and systemic and existential risks.

For each category, the analysis is grounded in documented evidence: published accident records, peer-reviewed security research, economic data from authoritative sources, and established psychological and sociological literature. Risks that lack an evidence base are identified as such and assessed separately from risks with documented precedent.

The chapter concludes with a risk matrix plotting likelihood against impact for all identified risks, providing a decision-relevant summary that complements the benefits analysis in Chapter 13. Risk without magnitude and probability is not useful information. Risk with both is a foundation for design, policy, and governance.

An important preliminary observation: the risk categories examined here are not unique to humanoid robots. Many apply to autonomous vehicles, drone systems, and conventional industrial robots. What distinguishes humanoid robots is the concentration of multiple risk vectors in a single platform: a humanoid robot present in a domestic environment simultaneously introduces physical safety, cybersecurity, privacy, and psychological risks in ways that a single-purpose autonomous system (a self-driving car, an industrial robot arm) does not.

## 14.2 Physical Safety Risks

### 14.2.1 The Fundamental Physics of a Biped

The physical safety risk profile of a humanoid robot begins with basic mechanics. A commercial humanoid robot platform weighs approximately 40–80 kg, stands 1.2–1.8 m tall, and is capable of applying forces at its end effectors in the range of 50–200 N depending on design. A robot of this size and mass, falling uncontrolled from standing height onto a person, imparts kinetic energy in the range of 500–1000 joules — comparable to being struck by a heavy object dropped from height. At this energy level, serious injury is a credible outcome.

This is not a hypothetical scenario. Bipedal locomotion is inherently unstable. Even the most capable research humanoid robots as of 2024 — Boston Dynamics' Atlas, Tesla's Optimus Gen 2 — fall during demonstrations in unstructured environments. The DARPA Robotics Challenge, described in Chapter 13, documented falls as a routine occurrence across all competing platforms [1]. Unlike a wheeled robot that tips over at low center of mass, a falling humanoid undergoes an angular momentum trajectory that can bring limbs and torso into contact with nearby humans or objects at substantial velocity.

### 14.2.2 Actuator Failure Scenarios

Humanoid robots are driven by electrical actuators — servo motors, linear actuators, or hydraulic systems — that are subject to mechanical failure modes. The failure modes of greatest concern from a safety perspective are:

**Uncontrolled actuator release:** If a joint actuator loses power or experiences a software fault, the limb it controls may fall under gravity. An arm actuator failure with a grasped load could drop that load onto a nearby person. A leg actuator failure during locomotion would precipitate an uncontrolled fall.

**Uncontrolled torque application:** A software fault in a torque-controlled joint could cause the joint to apply maximum torque unexpectedly — a grasping hand that closes with full force, a shoulder joint that rotates with full power. The forces involved are sufficient to cause fractures in human limbs.

**Thermal runaway in battery systems:** Lithium-ion battery packs, the energy source for current commercial humanoid robots, are subject to thermal runaway failure modes. The same failure mode responsible for recalls of consumer electronics and electric vehicle fires applies to humanoid robot battery systems.

These failure modes are not merely theoretical. Industrial robot arm failures resulting in worker injuries are documented in occupational safety records. OSHA's database of robot-related fatalities and injuries shows that the majority of incidents involve unintended robot motion due to programming error, sensor failure, or maintenance errors [2].

### 14.2.3 ISO 10218 and ISO/TS 15066: Standards and Their Limits

Industrial robot safety is governed primarily by ISO 10218-1 (Safety requirements for industrial robots — Robots) and ISO 10218-2 (Safety requirements for industrial robots — Robot systems and integration), which set requirements for robot design, safeguarding, and installation [3]. For collaborative robot operations where humans and robots share workspace, ISO/TS 15066 defines power and force limiting requirements: the maximum permissible contact forces between robot and human body regions, ranging from 35 N for the skull to 110 N for the thorax [4].

These standards were developed for industrial robot arms operating in defined workspaces with predictable human approach patterns. Their application to general-purpose humanoid robots operating in domestic or public environments is fundamentally limited for several reasons:

First, the standards assume a defined operational zone where the robot operates and human entry can be controlled or detected. A domestic humanoid robot has no such zone — it operates throughout the home, including in close proximity to vulnerable individuals who may not be able to respond to warning signals.

Second, ISO/TS 15066's power and force limits were derived from biomechanical studies of transient, non-injurious contact between robot arms and adult workers. They do not address the impact dynamics of a falling humanoid robot, the risks to children or elderly people with reduced bone density, or the cumulative effects of repeated low-force contact.

Third, the conformity assessment process for ISO 10218 assumes a relatively stable robot morphology and operational context. A humanoid robot that can be updated with new software behavior presents a dynamic conformity challenge that the current standards framework does not fully address.

The standards gap for humanoid robots in non-industrial settings is an active area of work within IEC/ISO committees, but as of 2025, no comprehensive safety standard equivalent to ISO 10218 exists specifically for general-purpose humanoid robots in domestic and public environments.

### 14.2.4 Documented Industrial Robot Accidents

The historical record of industrial robot accidents provides a sobering baseline for humanoid robot safety evaluation. OSHA's reporting requirement for severe injuries means that fatal and hospitalization-causing robot incidents are documented, though near-miss incidents are systematically underreported.

A 2014 analysis of OSHA records identified 144 robot-related accidents in US industry between 1984 and 2013, resulting in 36 fatalities [5]. The majority involved maintenance workers who had entered robot operating zones — scenarios that humanoid robot deployment in shared human environments would generalize. A robot welder at a Volkswagen assembly plant in Germany fatally struck a technician in 2015 when the worker entered the robot's operating zone; the incident highlighted the inadequacy of safety protocols when robots operate near humans [6].

While humanoid robots are designed with closer human proximity as an explicit operating parameter, the historical accident record demonstrates that robot safety incidents occur even when safety systems are present and protocols are in place. The transition from industrial robot arms to humanoid robots changes the physical configuration but does not eliminate the fundamental hazard of powerful mechanical systems operating near humans.

### 14.2.5 Safety by Design Principles

The engineering response to these risks involves several design principles that are actively being implemented in commercial humanoid platforms:

**Compliance and impedance control:** Rather than position-controlled joints that apply force until the commanded position is reached, torque-controlled joints can be configured with compliance — they yield when they encounter unexpected resistance, limiting the force applied. Series elastic actuators (SEAs) physically incorporate compliance through elastic elements in the drivetrain.

**Collision detection:** Force-torque sensors and current monitoring in joint actuators can detect unexpected contact and trigger a controlled stop or compliant response. Boston Dynamics, Figure AI, and others implement such systems, though their reliability and response latency in real-world conditions remain areas of active development.

**Whole-body motion planning:** Rather than planning robot motions without regard for nearby humans, whole-body planners that continuously model the robot's proximity to humans and adjust trajectories to maintain safe separation represent a software-layer safety approach.

These principles are real and being implemented. Their adequacy for a general-purpose robot operating in an uncontrolled domestic environment — where a toddler may run into the robot's path, where an elderly person may lose balance and fall against it — is not yet established by documented deployment evidence.

## 14.3 Cybersecurity Threats

### 14.3.1 The Robot Operating System Attack Surface

The dominant software framework for robot development, ROS 2 (Robot Operating System 2), presents a cybersecurity attack surface that has been analyzed in published academic research. ROS 2 uses the Data Distribution Service (DDS) middleware for communication between robot software components. The DDS standard was designed for reliability and performance in industrial automation; its security model was not originally designed for adversarial network environments.

Research published by Alias Robotics in 2020 documented that in its default configuration, ROS 2 does not require authentication for inter-node communication, does not encrypt messages, and does not enforce access control between different software components [7]. This means that any device on the same network as a robot can, without credentials, publish commands to any ROS 2 topic — including control commands that direct actuator motion.

The practical consequence is that a robot running default ROS 2 configuration, connected to a home or enterprise network, presents an unauthenticated command injection surface. An attacker on the same network (or who has penetrated the network) can issue movement commands to the robot's actuators.

### 14.3.2 Network Scanning and Exposed ROS 2 Nodes

The security research firm Alias Robotics conducted systematic internet scanning for exposed ROS 2 nodes and published findings indicating hundreds of ROS and ROS 2 nodes accessible from the public internet — research and industrial robots with no authentication barrier between the public internet and their control infrastructure [8]. While most of these represent research setups rather than commercial deployments, the finding demonstrates that the security posture of the ROS ecosystem does not currently meet standards appropriate for robots operating in proximity to humans.

The DDS Security specification (DDS-Security v1.1) provides optional authentication, encryption, and access control for ROS 2, but enabling it requires explicit configuration by the system integrator and imposes computational overhead. In practice, many deployments — particularly in research contexts — do not enable DDS security, a finding consistent with the scanning results.

### 14.3.3 Remote Takeover via MITM Teleoperation

Several commercial and research humanoid robot platforms include teleoperation capabilities — allowing a human operator to remotely control the robot, typically for tasks that exceed autonomous capability or for remote inspection. The communication channel between teleoperator and robot represents a man-in-the-middle attack surface: an attacker who can intercept and inject commands into the teleoperation stream can control the robot.

Common teleoperation protocols, if implemented without end-to-end authenticated encryption, are vulnerable to this attack. Given the physical consequences of unauthorized control of a 50 kg biped — the robot can be directed to strike, restrain, or obstruct people — the severity of a successful teleoperation MITM attack is significantly greater than the equivalent attack against, for example, a telepresence screen.

A 2022 security analysis of commercial telepresence robot platforms found that multiple products used unencrypted or weakly authenticated communication channels for control signals, despite the fact that these robots were deployed in healthcare and business environments [9].

### 14.3.4 Adversarial Attacks on Vision Systems

Humanoid robots that rely on computer vision for navigation, object recognition, and human detection are vulnerable to adversarial perturbations — carefully crafted physical inputs designed to cause misclassification in neural network-based vision systems.

The physical adversarial patch attack, introduced in academic research by Brown et al. in 2017, demonstrated that printed patterns, when placed in a scene, could cause object detection networks to systematically fail to detect specific objects [10]. Applied to humanoid robot vision, this attack could cause a robot's person-detection system to fail to recognize a human in its path — with physical collision consequences — or to fail to recognize a specific individual's face for access control purposes.

More concerning for robots that accept visual instructions, deepfake video commands represent an emerging attack vector: a robot that responds to visual input from its cameras (such as gesture-based commands or reading visual instructions from printed materials) could be provided with adversarially crafted visual inputs that trigger unintended actions.

### 14.3.5 VLA Prompt Injection

The most forward-looking cybersecurity threat for AI humanoid robots is vision-language-action (VLA) model prompt injection. Contemporary humanoid robot control architectures increasingly use large language model (LLM) or vision-language model (VLM) components for high-level planning and instruction following. A robot that interprets voice commands or reads text instructions via a language model is potentially vulnerable to prompt injection attacks analogous to those documented for text-based LLM deployments.

Prompt injection in text LLMs has been extensively documented: a user who can inject text into the model's context can override system instructions, change the model's behavior, and cause it to follow attacker-controlled directives [11]. Applied to a humanoid robot with a VLA architecture, malicious voice commands or visual text inputs (a sign in the robot's field of view) could potentially override the robot's task instructions and redirect its physical actions.

This is an active area of research with significant implications. The 2024 paper by Perez et al. on jailbreaking physical LLM-controlled robots demonstrated that prompt injection attacks could cause LLM-controlled robotic arms to ignore safety constraints and perform prohibited actions when adversarially crafted text was included in their task context [12]. While this research was conducted on arm robots, the principles apply directly to any humanoid robot using LLM-based high-level control.

## 14.4 Privacy Risks

### 14.4.1 Always-On Sensors in Domestic Environments

A humanoid robot operating in a domestic environment is equipped with cameras, microphones, depth sensors, and potentially thermal imaging — all active throughout its operational period. Unlike a stationary home security camera with a fixed field of view, a mobile humanoid robot with a 360-degree perception system provides comprehensive environmental coverage from a platform that moves throughout the space.

The data generated by this sensor array is qualitatively different from data generated by other smart home devices. A smart speaker records audio in response to a wake word. A robot records continuous video and audio, including the spatial layout of the home, the identities and behavioral patterns of occupants, visitor presence and frequency, the contents of rooms, and the physical routines of household members. The aggregation of this data over time creates an intimate behavioral profile that substantially exceeds what any other consumer device generates.

### 14.4.2 GDPR Compliance Challenges

Under the European Union's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), the collection and processing of personal data requires a lawful basis (Article 6), special category data (including biometric data for unique identification — Article 9) requires explicit consent or another statutory basis, and data subjects retain rights including access, rectification, erasure, and objection [13].

The application of GDPR to domestic robots raises several compliance challenges that have not been resolved in regulatory guidance:

**Identity of the controller:** GDPR requires identification of a data controller responsible for processing decisions. In a domestic robot deployment, is the controller the manufacturer (who determines the system's data processing capabilities), the software platform operator (who may process data in the cloud), or the household user (who chooses to deploy the robot)? Current GDPR guidance does not clearly address this multi-party data controller scenario for consumer robots.

**Consent from non-users:** A household's robot operates in the presence of visitors, domestic workers, children, and others who are not the robot's purchaser and have not provided consent for their data to be collected. GDPR's consent requirements apply to all data subjects, not merely the party who operates the device.

**Data minimization:** GDPR Article 5(1)(c) requires that personal data be "adequate, relevant and limited to what is necessary in relation to the purposes for which they are processed." A robot collecting continuous full-environment video for navigation purposes — where the navigation function may require only depth maps and obstacle locations — may collect substantially more personal data than is strictly necessary for its stated purpose.

**Data retention and deletion:** GDPR Article 5(1)(e) requires that data be "kept in a form which permits identification of data subjects for no longer than is necessary." The default data retention and deletion practices of robot platform operators are not consistently documented in publicly accessible privacy policies.

### 14.4.3 Data Sovereignty and Corporate Surveillance

Beyond the immediate GDPR compliance questions, the data sovereignty question is of structural concern. Who owns the data collected by a domestic humanoid robot? Manufacturer terms of service for smart home devices have historically claimed broad rights to use collected data for product improvement, analytics, and in some cases third-party sharing [14]. Applied to the substantially more intimate data generated by a domestic humanoid robot, these terms of service models raise serious questions about the effective privacy rights of households that deploy such systems.

The concern is not hypothetical. Amazon's Ring doorbell ecosystem, the subject of a Federal Trade Commission settlement in 2023, was found to have provided Ring video footage to law enforcement without customer consent and to have given employees broad access to customer video data [15]. The data generated by a domestic humanoid robot — which includes far more intimate information than doorbell footage — creates a surveillance infrastructure of a qualitatively different order.

The risk is that the deployment of domestic humanoid robots will, through widely-accepted terms of service, effectively transfer detailed behavioral surveillance of private households to technology companies, with limited regulatory constraint and negligible consumer awareness of the terms agreed to at the time of purchase.

## 14.5 Economic Disruption

### 14.5.1 Job Displacement: Sector Analysis

The economic argument against humanoid robots — that they will displace human workers at a pace and scale that disrupts employment — must be evaluated with the same analytical care as the economic argument for them. The historical record of technology-driven employment disruption shows complex outcomes: some workers in directly automated occupations face wage pressure and displacement, while employment grows in complementary occupations and new sectors. But the distributional effects — who bears the cost of displacement — are not uniform.

The International Federation of Robotics reports that the global installed base of industrial robots grew from approximately 1.8 million in 2016 to 3.9 million in 2022 — more than doubling over six years [16]. A study by Acemoglu and Restrepo, published in the *Journal of Political Economy*, found that each additional robot per 1,000 workers reduced the employment-to-population ratio in commuting zones by 0.2 percentage points and wages by approximately 0.42% [17]. These are real effects, though their magnitude is debated, and they are concentrated in communities dependent on the specific tasks being automated.

For humanoid robots, the International Monetary Fund projected in early 2024 that AI-related automation could affect 40% of global employment, with advanced economies more exposed (60%) due to higher concentrations of cognitive service jobs [18]. The IMF analysis noted that in many cases, AI will "complement" rather than replace workers — but that the complementarity primarily benefits higher-skilled workers while lower-skilled workers in directly automated tasks face net negative effects.

### 14.5.2 Manufacturing Displacement

Manufacturing is the sector where displacement by robots is most extensively documented. The Acemoglu-Restrepo analysis focused specifically on industrial robot adoption in manufacturing and found statistically robust negative employment effects in directly affected occupations, without proportional compensation in new manufacturing employment [17].

The extension to humanoid robots matters because humanoid robots, unlike fixed-arm industrial robots, can potentially substitute for the general assembly labor — tasks that require some degree of flexibility and physical dexterity — that has historically been difficult to automate and has therefore retained human employment even as more routine manufacturing tasks were automated. If humanoid robots achieve the flexible assembly capability their proponents project, the population of manufacturing workers exposed to displacement will be substantially larger than was previously the case.

### 14.5.3 Service Sector: Hospitality, Cleaning, Delivery

The service sector — where humanoid robots are also projected to deploy — employs a large population of workers in roles characterized by relatively low wages, limited formal education requirements, and high physical demands. Hotel housekeeping, commercial cleaning, food delivery, and retail stocking are all tasks for which humanoid robot capability is advancing. The workers in these roles have fewer alternative employment options than manufacturing workers displaced by automation in previous decades.

McKinsey Global Institute projections estimate that 50–85% of cleaning work hours, 40–75% of food preparation and service hours, and 45–70% of basic physical sales work could technically be automated with current or near-term robotic technology [19]. The technical feasibility of automation does not determine its pace — economic viability, regulatory environment, and social acceptance all moderate actual deployment rates — but it establishes the outer boundary of exposure.

### 14.5.4 Unequal Access and Concentration of Power

Beyond the direct displacement effect, the deployment pattern of humanoid robots raises a distributional concern: robots are expensive capital equipment that benefits those who can afford to deploy them. If humanoid robots produce the productivity gains their proponents project, those gains accrue primarily to capital owners — the shareholders of robot manufacturers and the firms that deploy robots — rather than to the workers who are displaced.

The risk is a form of technological enclosure: a productivity gain that, in the absence of redistributive policy mechanisms (tax treatment of robot labor, revenue sharing, universal basic income schemes, or equivalent mechanisms), concentrates economic gains among those who already hold capital. This concern is explicitly acknowledged in the IMF's 2024 analysis of AI's economic effects, which noted that "without policy intervention, AI could worsen inequality" [18].

The humanoid robot deployment scenario is not unprecedented in this respect — the same distributional concern applies to all forms of capital-intensive automation. But the scale and pace of projected humanoid robot deployment makes the magnitude of the potential distributional effect significant.

## 14.6 Psychological and Social Risks

### 14.6.1 The Uncanny Valley

The concept of the uncanny valley, introduced by roboticist Masahiro Mori in 1970 [20], describes an empirically observed phenomenon: human responses to robots and other human-like entities become increasingly positive as the entity becomes more human-like — up to a point, beyond which a region of strongly negative responses (eeriness, discomfort, revulsion) is observed before positive responses resume at near-perfect human likeness. The valley in the term refers to this region of negative response.

Mori's original publication was a conceptual proposal based on his engineering intuitions rather than empirical research. Subsequent experimental work has provided partial empirical support for the uncanny valley effect, while also revealing significant individual variation in susceptibility and questioning the universality of the originally proposed curve [21]. A 2011 meta-analysis by Kätsyri et al. found evidence for an uncanny valley effect in experimental settings, particularly for dynamic, moving stimuli, but noted that the effect was less consistent across studies than the concept's influence in robot design would suggest [22].

For humanoid robot deployment, the uncanny valley raises design and acceptance questions rather than a clear harm. Robots designed to be clearly robotic — like Boston Dynamics' Atlas in its current form, which makes no attempt at human likeness — avoid the valley by not approaching human appearance. Robots designed for social interaction, like humanoid companion robots aimed at the elderly care market, must navigate this phenomenon carefully.

### 14.6.2 Emotional Dependency

The risk of emotional dependency on robots is more concrete and better-documented than the uncanny valley effect. Research on human-robot interaction has consistently demonstrated that people form parasocial relationships with robots — attributing mental states, forming emotional attachments, and modifying their behavior based on perceived robot feelings — even when they intellectually understand that the robot is not sentient [23].

For elderly users in particular — who may be socially isolated, cognitively impaired, or seeking companionship in institutional care settings — robot companions designed to elicit emotional engagement may produce genuine emotional dependency. The ethical concern is not that the robot causes emotional harm directly, but that emotional dependency on a robot may substitute for human relationships and reduce motivation to maintain or develop human social connections.

This concern is not uniformly supported by research evidence: some studies of companion robots with elderly populations have found that robot interaction supplements rather than substitutes for human interaction [24]. But the design choices embedded in companion robots — particularly for-profit companion robots with business models that reward maximizing engagement — create incentive structures that may not align with users' long-term wellbeing.

### 14.6.3 Anthropomorphization and Its Consequences

The tendency to anthropomorphize — to attribute human characteristics, intentions, and agency to non-human entities — is a well-documented feature of human cognition that operates even when people know the entity is not human [25]. Applied to humanoid robots, anthropomorphization raises several specific concerns:

**Misattribution of competence:** People who anthropomorphize a robot may attribute greater competence and reliability to it than its actual performance justifies, leading to over-reliance on robot judgment in situations where human oversight is necessary.

**Inappropriate emotional response to "robot harm":** People who anthropomorphize robots may experience distress when robots are powered down, reprogrammed, or physically damaged — responses that may be psychologically burdensome and that may complicate decisions about robot management.

**Exploitation of anthropomorphic responses:** Commercial actors who design humanoid robots to maximize anthropomorphic responses — to be perceived as emotional, vulnerable, or attached — may be exploiting users' psychological tendencies in ways that do not serve users' interests.

### 14.6.4 Children's Development

The effect of robot interaction on children's social and cognitive development is an active research area with preliminary but concerning findings. A systematic review published in *Computers in Human Behavior* in 2023 found that children interacted with social robots using social scripts similar to those they use with humans, and that extended robot interaction could affect children's expectations about social interaction reciprocity [26].

The concern is not that occasional robot interaction is harmful — the evidence does not support this — but that extended reliance on robot interaction during developmental periods when children are learning the norms and skills of human-to-human interaction may shape expectations in ways that are not yet understood. This is an area where precautionary principles are appropriate given the limited evidence base and the irreversibility of developmental influences.

### 14.6.5 Erosion of Human-to-Human Connection

At the societal level, the deployment of humanoid robots in social roles — care, companionship, service — substitutes robot-mediated interaction for human-to-human interaction. For the care sector specifically, if robotic assistants take on the physical tasks of care while human caregivers are freed for "higher-level" interaction, the result may be that human caregivers interact with fewer care recipients for shorter periods, reducing the aggregate amount of human-to-human social contact available to vulnerable populations.

This is a structural concern rather than a direct harm claim. The design and implementation choices around humanoid robot deployment in care settings will determine whether robots supplement or substitute for human social contact. The concern is that commercial and resource pressures may drive substitution even when the stated intent is supplementation.

## 14.7 Systemic and Existential Risks

### 14.7.1 Autonomous Weapons Potential

Humanoid robots designed for commercial and care applications are, physically, capable platforms for weaponization. A robot that can carry loads, navigate arbitrary terrain, operate doors and vehicles, and respond to visual and audio commands from a remote operator is, from a capabilities perspective, a functional autonomous weapons platform pending software configuration.

The dual-use concern — that commercially developed humanoid robot platforms are substantially equivalent in capability to military autonomous ground systems — is noted in defense policy analyses. The US Department of Defense Instruction 3000.09, updated in 2023, establishes requirements for human judgment in decisions to employ lethal force by autonomous systems [27]. These requirements govern US military systems but do not apply to commercial robot platforms that could be weaponized by non-state actors or foreign militaries.

The concern is not that commercial humanoid robots will spontaneously become weapons. The concern is that the same technology base — dexterous manipulation, mobile navigation, visual target detection — that enables a humanoid robot to sort packages in a warehouse enables a weaponized variant to be produced with relatively modest additional development. The barriers to this transition are primarily social, legal, and economic, rather than technical.

### 14.7.2 The Governance Vacuum

The most concrete systemic risk as of 2025 is not the risks described above individually, but the absence of a regulatory framework adequate to govern them collectively. Unlike pharmaceuticals (governed by pre-market approval frameworks), nuclear materials (governed by the International Atomic Energy Agency and national regulatory bodies), and aviation (governed by ICAO standards and national certification processes), humanoid robots face no mandatory pre-deployment safety certification, no international governance framework, and no consistent national regulatory requirements.

The EU AI Act, which entered into force in 2024 and whose high-risk provisions will begin applying in 2026, classifies AI systems used in safety-critical contexts under Annex III, which includes robotics applications that pose safety risks. But the AI Act is an AI governance framework, not a comprehensive robot safety framework — it addresses the AI components of a robot system, not the physical safety of the mechanical system as a whole [28].

Chapter 15 addresses the governance framework question in detail. The present observation is that the risks documented in this chapter are currently being accumulated without commensurate regulatory oversight — a situation that, in other industries with equivalent safety stakes, would be considered unacceptable.

### 14.7.3 Loss of Human Agency

At the most abstract level, the widespread deployment of humanoid robots in decision-supporting roles — advisors, assistants, caregivers — raises the question of human agency. If the physical and logistical management of healthcare, eldercare, education, and service industries is increasingly mediated by robotic systems, the human relationships and judgments embedded in those interactions are progressively displaced.

This is not an argument against automation in principle. It is an argument for deliberate choices about which aspects of human life should be mediated by machines and which should be preserved as human-to-human interactions, and for a governance framework that makes these choices explicitly rather than allowing them to be made by default through the accumulation of commercial deployment decisions.

## 14.9 Chapter Summary

The six risk categories in this chapter do not have equal urgency, equal evidence bases, or equal time horizons. Conflating near-term documented risks with long-term speculative ones is how risk analysis produces either panic or complacency, and this chapter has tried to avoid both.

The near-term, high-evidence risks are cybersecurity and privacy. ROS 2's default network exposure is a documented vulnerability with published CVEs and active scanning by security researchers. A domestic robot with continuous cameras and microphones creates an intimate surveillance capability that existing GDPR frameworks and corporate data practices have not been designed to constrain. Physical safety risks follow closely: the ISO standards gap between industrial and domestic robot deployment is a real regulatory failure, and the analogy to industrial robot accident history suggests the consequences of leaving it unaddressed.

Economic disruption from humanoid automation is real and will be uneven — the Acemoglu-Restrepo analysis and IFR deployment data support meaningful displacement in manufacturing and lower-wage service sectors. The distributional concern is not speculative; it is already observable in the populations most exposed to earlier waves of industrial automation. Psychological risks in care and companion contexts are supported by experimental research even where population-level evidence has not yet accumulated. Systemic risks — weaponization and the governance vacuum — represent the category where the evidence base is thinnest and the stakes are potentially highest, which is precisely the argument for building governance frameworks before harmful deployment patterns are established rather than after.

This analysis is not an argument against deployment. It is an argument for the conditions deployment requires: safety certification standards appropriate to mobile domestic systems, mandatory cybersecurity baselines, privacy-preserving architecture requirements, distributional policy instruments, and a regulatory framework built for the capabilities humanoid robots actually have. Chapter 15 proposes that framework.
