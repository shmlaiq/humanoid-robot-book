## 11.1 Introduction — From Perception and Language to Physical Action

A humanoid robot standing in a kitchen must accomplish something no classical AI system was ever asked to do: it must see a cluttered countertop, hear the words "put the blue bowl in the dish rack," understand that the blue bowl is the object on the left, navigate to it, grasp it without crushing it, and carry it to the correct location — all within a few seconds, in response to a sentence it has never encountered before.

Each of those sub-tasks individually is a research problem. Computer vision researchers spent decades perfecting object detection. Natural language processing researchers spent decades building systems that could parse and understand human speech. Robotics researchers spent decades designing controllers that could grasp objects reliably. Yet for most of AI history, these three threads ran in parallel, each advancing rapidly but separately.

The **Vision-Language-Action (VLA) model** is the architecture that joins them.

A VLA model is a single end-to-end neural network that takes as input both visual observations — images from the robot's cameras — and language instructions, and produces as output motor commands: the precise joint angles, velocities, or forces that cause the robot to act. Rather than building a perception module, a language module, and a control module separately and stitching them together with hand-written interfaces, a VLA model learns all three simultaneously from data.

This architectural unification is not merely an engineering convenience. When perception, language, and action are trained jointly, something remarkable emerges: the model develops shared representations that connect what it sees to what it knows to what it can do. It can follow instructions about objects it learned about from web text even if it has never encountered those objects in robot training data. It can reason about spatial relationships because it has learned to read about them. It can generalize to novel scenes because its vision encoder was pretrained on billions of images.

This chapter traces the VLA architecture from its theoretical foundations through the three systems that have defined the field: RT-2 from Google DeepMind [1], OpenVLA from UC Berkeley [2], and π0 from Physical Intelligence [3]. We examine the mechanisms by which these systems work, their demonstrated capabilities, their fundamental limitations, and the engineering choices that distinguish them from one another.

## 11.2 Architecture of VLA Models

Understanding VLA systems requires first understanding the three functional components that every VLA model must possess: a **vision encoder** that converts camera images into numerical representations, a **language encoder** that converts instruction text into compatible representations, and an **action head** that converts the combined representations into robot motor commands. These three components are joined by a **fusion mechanism** that allows visual and linguistic information to inform each other.

### 11.2.1 The Encoder-Decoder-Action Head Structure

The canonical VLA architecture follows a pattern familiar from the broader field of multimodal deep learning. An image `I` captured by the robot's camera and an instruction string `l` provided by a human user are the inputs. The desired output is an action vector `a` that can be executed by the robot's actuators.

Formally:

```
a = ActionHead(Fuse(VisionEncoder(I), LanguageEncoder(l)))
```

In practice, this diagram is more nuanced. Modern VLA systems typically build on a pretrained **Vision-Language Model (VLM)** — a large neural network already trained to understand images and text together — and add a lightweight action head on top. The VLM provides the heavy representational lifting; the action head translates those representations into the language of robot joints.

### 11.2.2 The Vision Encoder: Vision Transformer (ViT)

The **Vision Transformer (ViT)**, introduced by Dosovitskiy et al. in 2020 [4], has become the standard vision backbone for VLA systems. Unlike convolutional neural networks that process images through local filters, ViT divides an image into a grid of fixed-size patches (typically 16×16 pixels), linearly embeds each patch into a high-dimensional vector, and processes the sequence of patch embeddings with a standard transformer encoder.

The key insight of ViT is that images can be treated as sequences of tokens — just like words in a sentence. This makes them directly compatible with transformer-based language models, which also process sequences of tokens. When a VLA model needs to fuse visual and linguistic information, the shared transformer architecture makes this fusion architecturally natural rather than requiring custom bridging components.

For a 224×224 image with 16×16 patches, ViT produces a sequence of 196 patch embeddings plus one learnable `[CLS]` token, yielding 197 visual tokens. These tokens carry spatial information about the image: the model can learn which patches correspond to objects, surfaces, and spatial relationships.

In the VLA context, the vision encoder is typically the **SigLIP** [5] or **CLIP** [6] vision encoder — variants of ViT pretrained by aligning image representations with text representations on hundreds of millions of image-caption pairs from the internet. This pretraining ensures that the visual tokens already encode semantically meaningful information: the visual token for "red mug" is spatially close to the text token for "red mug" in the embedding space.

### 11.2.3 The Language Encoder

The language encoder converts the human instruction string — "pick up the cube and place it in the bin" — into a sequence of contextual token embeddings. In early VLA systems, this was a BERT-style bidirectional encoder [7] that produced a fixed-length representation of the entire instruction. In modern systems, it is typically a large language model (LLM) such as Llama 2 [8] or a GPT-style autoregressive decoder.

The choice between BERT-style encoders and LLM decoders has significant consequences. BERT-style encoders are fast and produce compact representations, but they are limited to understanding instructions that closely resemble their training distribution. LLM decoders, being trained on vast amounts of internet text, bring far richer world knowledge: they understand that a "dish rack" is distinct from a "dish washer," that "the heavier one" requires comparative reasoning, and that "near the sink" implies a spatial relationship.

RT-2's key innovation was to use a full LLM (specifically a vision-language model based on PaLI-X) as both the language encoder and the action decoder, enabling it to leverage the full richness of web-scale language pretraining.

### 11.2.4 The Action Decoder and Action Head

The action head is the component that distinguishes a VLA model from a standard VLM. Given the fused visual-linguistic representation, the action head must produce a concrete motor command: typically a 7-dimensional vector for a standard 6-DOF robot arm with gripper, specifying `[Δx, Δy, Δz, Δroll, Δpitch, Δyaw, gripper_open]` — a delta in end-effector pose plus a gripper command.

There are three primary architectural approaches to implementing the action head:

**1. Regression head.** A simple multi-layer perceptron (MLP) added on top of the frozen or fine-tuned VLM backbone, directly regressing continuous action values. This is the simplest approach but tends to produce jerky, unstable motions on out-of-distribution inputs.

**2. Tokenized action head (RT-2 style).** Actions are discretized into bins and represented as tokens from the language model's vocabulary. The LLM then "predicts" actions the same way it predicts the next word in a sentence. This approach enables joint training on language and action data, which is the key enabler of RT-2's emergent capabilities.

**3. Diffusion / flow matching head (π0 style).** An auxiliary network learns to generate actions by iteratively refining a noise vector toward a valid action trajectory. This produces smooth, high-fidelity motor commands suitable for dexterous manipulation, but adds inference complexity.

### 11.2.5 Token Fusion: How Visual and Language Tokens Are Combined

The fusion of visual tokens and language tokens occurs inside the transformer's self-attention layers. The standard approach is **concatenation followed by self-attention**: the sequence of visual patch tokens is concatenated with the sequence of language tokens to form a single mixed-sequence input to the transformer. Every token can attend to every other token, allowing the model to associate specific words ("blue," "left," "heavy") with specific image patches (the blue object, the left side of the scene, the larger object).

More sophisticated approaches use **cross-attention**: the language tokens are processed by a separate encoder, and their representations are injected into the vision encoder's layers via cross-attention operations. This allows the visual processing to be conditioned on the instruction at every layer, rather than only at the fusion stage.

In practice, systems like OpenVLA use token concatenation because it is simpler and compatible with standard transformer implementations. The visual tokens from the SigLIP encoder are projected into the LLM's embedding space and prepended to the tokenized instruction, forming a single input sequence that the LLM processes autoregressively.

## 11.3 RT-2: Robotics Transformer 2

### 11.3.1 Background and Motivation

RT-2, published by Brohan et al. at Google DeepMind in 2023 [1], represents the most influential VLA system to date. Its core insight was deceptively simple: if a vision-language model trained on the internet knows about the world — knows that "Coke can" is a container of dark liquid, that "most recyclable" refers to an environmental property, and that these facts imply the Coke can should go in the recycling bin — then a robot controlled by such a model should be able to act on that knowledge even if no robot training example ever demonstrated the "put recyclable items in recycling" behavior.

The key challenge was making the robot training task compatible with the VLM training task, so that both could be learned by the same neural network parameters.

### 11.3.2 Key Innovation: Co-Fine-Tuning on Web Data and Robot Demonstrations

RT-2's architecture builds on PaLI-X [9], a 55-billion parameter vision-language model trained by Google on web-scale image-text data. Rather than using PaLI-X as a fixed feature extractor, the RT-2 team fine-tuned the entire model jointly on two datasets:

- **Web data:** The standard VLM training mixture (image-text pairs, question-answering datasets, visual reasoning benchmarks)
- **Robot demonstration data:** ~130,000 episodes of a robot arm performing tabletop manipulation tasks, collected using Google's RT-1 infrastructure

This **co-fine-tuning** strategy was the critical technical contribution. Previous approaches had used pretrained VLMs as frozen encoders and trained only a robot-specific head on top. Co-fine-tuning allowed the model's internal representations to be shaped simultaneously by both distributions, preventing the model from forgetting its world knowledge while learning robot-specific behaviors.

The training objective unified both tasks: for web data, predict the next token (standard language modeling); for robot data, predict the next action token (using the tokenized action representation described below).

### 11.3.3 Action Tokenization in RT-2

The central technical mechanism enabling RT-2's co-fine-tuning is **action tokenization**: the representation of continuous robot actions as discrete tokens drawn from the language model's vocabulary.

A standard 7-DOF robot arm action is a vector of 7 continuous values, each representing a joint displacement or end-effector delta. To convert this into a token sequence compatible with language model training, RT-2 applies uniform discretization: each of the 7 action dimensions is divided into 256 bins, and a single integer bin index becomes the "action token" for that dimension.

These 256 bin indices are mapped onto existing tokens in the language model's vocabulary — specifically, the 256 least-used tokens in the PaLM vocabulary are repurposed as action tokens. This design means the action prediction task is structurally identical to next-token prediction: the model autoregressively predicts 7 tokens, one per action dimension, in sequence.

The full RT-2 inference procedure is:

1. Encode the camera image(s) into visual tokens via the ViT vision encoder
2. Tokenize the instruction string into text tokens
3. Concatenate visual tokens and text tokens
4. Autoregressively decode 7 action tokens
5. De-tokenize: convert each integer bin index back to a continuous value in the original action range
6. Execute the resulting 7-DOF action on the robot

### 11.3.4 Emergent Capabilities

The most striking result in the RT-2 paper was not performance on standard benchmarks — though RT-2 improved on those — but the emergence of **generalization capabilities** that no amount of robot training data alone would have produced.

The RT-2 team designed evaluation tasks specifically intended to test knowledge that the model could only have acquired from web training:

- **Novel object generalization:** The model successfully manipulated objects (a toy airplane, a stapler) it had never seen in robot demonstrations, because it had seen those objects in web images
- **Reasoning about abstract properties:** Asked to "pick up the object that could serve as a drink for humans," the model identified a water bottle rather than a cup (which was empty in the scene)
- **Following multi-step reasoning chains:** Asked to "pick up the food that a lion would eat," the model identified the meat

These capabilities were compared between a version of RT-2 trained with co-fine-tuning and an ablated version trained on robot data only. The co-fine-tuned model succeeded on emergent reasoning tasks at approximately twice the rate.

### 11.3.5 RT-2 Limitations

RT-2's performance came with significant practical costs:

**Inference latency.** At 55B parameters, RT-2 produces actions at approximately 1–3 Hz on Google's specialized TPU hardware. Tasks requiring rapid response — catching a falling object, maintaining balance while walking — are impossible at this frequency. The slower RT-2-B variant (12B parameters) achieves ~10 Hz but with degraded generalization.

**Single-arm focus.** All RT-2 demonstrations involve a single 6-DOF robot arm on a fixed base. The architecture has not been demonstrated on bipedal humanoids or tasks requiring bimanual coordination.

**Training cost.** Co-fine-tuning a 55B parameter model requires Google-scale compute infrastructure. Reproducing RT-2 training is not feasible for most research groups.

**Proprietary system.** RT-2 weights are not publicly released, preventing independent reproduction and improvement.

## 11.4 OpenVLA: An Open-Source VLA System

### 11.4.1 Background and Motivation

The proprietary nature of RT-2 motivated researchers at UC Berkeley, Stanford, Carnegie Mellon, and other institutions to develop **OpenVLA** [2]: an open-source VLA system designed to be reproduced and extended by the research community. OpenVLA was released in 2024 with full model weights, training code, and evaluation infrastructure.

OpenVLA's design philosophy prioritized accessibility without sacrificing capability: train a VLA system of practical size (7B parameters) on publicly available data, using publicly available infrastructure, and achieve competitive performance with RT-2.

### 11.4.2 Architecture: Prismatic VLM Backbone

OpenVLA is built on the **Prismatic VLM** [10] framework, which combines:

- **SigLIP vision encoder** [5]: A Vision Transformer trained using sigmoid loss on image-text pairs, producing higher-quality visual representations than CLIP for fine-grained object understanding
- **Llama 2 language model** [8]: A 7B parameter LLM trained by Meta on publicly available web text, providing strong language understanding and generation capabilities

The fusion strategy follows the LLaVA pattern [11]: visual tokens from SigLIP are projected into Llama 2's embedding space via a two-layer MLP projector, then prepended to the tokenized instruction sequence. The combined sequence is processed by Llama 2's transformer layers autoregressively.

For action output, OpenVLA uses the same discretization approach as RT-2: each of the 7 action dimensions is discretized into 256 bins, mapped to 256 tokens from the Llama 2 vocabulary (the vocabulary tokens with indices 32,000–32,255 are repurposed as action bins), and predicted autoregressively.

### 11.4.3 Training Dataset: Open X-Embodiment

Where RT-2 trained on Google's proprietary robot demonstration data, OpenVLA trained on the **Open X-Embodiment (OXE)** dataset [12] — a community-assembled collection of robot demonstration data from 22 different research institutions, covering 21 different robot embodiments, comprising approximately 970,000 demonstration episodes.

OXE represents a landmark in the robotics community: an acknowledgment that no single institution can collect enough demonstration data to train generalizable robot policies, and that pooling data across institutions and robots is necessary. The breadth of OXE — demonstrations from Franka arms, UR5 arms, mobile manipulators, and others, performing tasks from tabletop grasping to door opening to object sorting — provides the diverse training signal that OpenVLA needs to learn generalizable behaviors.

### 11.4.4 Running OpenVLA Inference: Python Code Example

The following code demonstrates loading a pretrained OpenVLA model and running inference to generate an action from a camera image and an instruction. OpenVLA requires Python 3.10+, PyTorch 2.0+, and the `transformers` library from Hugging Face.

```python
# openvla_inference.py
# Requires: pip install transformers torch torchvision pillow
# OpenVLA model: openvla/openvla-7b (on HuggingFace Hub)

from typing import Optional
import torch
from PIL import Image
from transformers import AutoModelForVision2Seq, AutoProcessor
import numpy as np


class OpenVLAController:
    """
    Wrapper around the OpenVLA-7B model for robot action inference.

    The model takes a single camera image and a natural language instruction,
    and returns a 7-dimensional action vector:
        [delta_x, delta_y, delta_z,
         delta_roll, delta_pitch, delta_yaw,
         gripper_open]
    """

    # Action space bounds (meters for translation, radians for rotation)
    ACTION_DIM = 7
    ACTION_BINS = 256

    def __init__(
        self,
        model_name: str = "openvla/openvla-7b",
        device: str = "cuda",
        torch_dtype: torch.dtype = torch.bfloat16,
    ) -> None:
        self.device = device
        self.torch_dtype = torch_dtype

        print(f"Loading OpenVLA processor from {model_name}...")
        self.processor = AutoProcessor.from_pretrained(
            model_name,
            trust_remote_code=True,
        )

        print(f"Loading OpenVLA model ({torch_dtype}) on {device}...")
        self.model = AutoModelForVision2Seq.from_pretrained(
            model_name,
            torch_dtype=torch_dtype,
            low_cpu_mem_usage=True,
            trust_remote_code=True,
        ).to(device)

        self.model.eval()
        print("OpenVLA model loaded successfully.")

    @torch.inference_mode()
    def predict_action(
        self,
        image: Image.Image,
        instruction: str,
        unnorm_key: Optional[str] = "bridge_orig",
    ) -> np.ndarray:
        """
        Run VLA inference: image + instruction -> 7-DOF action vector.

        Args:
            image:        PIL Image from the robot's wrist or head camera.
            instruction:  Natural language task instruction.
            unnorm_key:   Dataset statistics key for action un-normalization.
                          Use "bridge_orig" for BridgeData-style tasks,
                          or None to return raw normalized actions.

        Returns:
            action: np.ndarray of shape (7,), representing
                    [dx, dy, dz, d_roll, d_pitch, d_yaw, gripper].
        """
        # Build the prompt in OpenVLA's expected format
        prompt = f"In: What action should the robot take to {instruction}?\nOut:"

        # Prepare inputs: image + prompt -> token IDs + pixel values
        inputs = self.processor(prompt, image).to(
            self.device, dtype=self.torch_dtype
        )

        # Generate 7 action tokens autoregressively
        # max_new_tokens=7: one token per action dimension
        action_tokens = self.model.generate(
            **inputs,
            max_new_tokens=self.ACTION_DIM,
            do_sample=False,   # Greedy decoding for deterministic actions
        )

        # Decode action tokens to a normalized action vector
        # The processor handles de-tokenization and un-normalization
        action = self.processor.decode_actions(
            action_tokens,
            unnorm_key=unnorm_key,
        )

        return action  # shape: (7,)

    def get_action_description(self, action: np.ndarray) -> str:
        """Return a human-readable description of an action vector."""
        labels = ["dx", "dy", "dz", "d_roll", "d_pitch", "d_yaw", "gripper"]
        parts = [f"{label}={val:+.4f}" for label, val in zip(labels, action)]
        return "Action: [" + ", ".join(parts) + "]"


def demo_openvla_inference() -> None:
    """
    Demonstration of OpenVLA inference on a single image-instruction pair.
    """
    # Instantiate the controller
    controller = OpenVLAController(
        model_name="openvla/openvla-7b",
        device="cuda" if torch.cuda.is_available() else "cpu",
    )

    # Load a sample image (replace with actual robot camera feed)
    image = Image.open("sample_camera_frame.jpg").convert("RGB")

    # Define the task instruction
    instruction = "pick up the blue cube and place it on the red plate"

    # Run inference
    print(f"\nInstruction: '{instruction}'")
    action = controller.predict_action(image, instruction)

    print(controller.get_action_description(action))
    print(f"\nRaw action array: {action}")
    print(f"Gripper command: {'OPEN' if action[-1] > 0.5 else 'CLOSE'}")


if __name__ == "__main__":
    demo_openvla_inference()
```

The model generates actions at approximately **6–8 Hz** on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU with bfloat16 precision, making it feasible for reactive manipulation tasks with moderately slow dynamics.

### 11.4.5 Performance Comparison: OpenVLA vs RT-2

In the OpenVLA paper's evaluations on the BridgeData V2 benchmark [13], OpenVLA-7B achieved a **76.5% success rate** on manipulation tasks, compared to RT-2's **77.3%** — a gap of less than one percentage point, despite OpenVLA having 87% fewer parameters (7B vs 55B) and being trained entirely on publicly available data.

This result demonstrated that the gap between open and closed VLA systems was far smaller than the parameter count difference might suggest, and that model scale was not the dominant factor in manipulation performance.

## 11.5 π0 (Pi-Zero) — Physical Intelligence

### 11.5.1 Background

π0, published by Black et al. at Physical Intelligence (π) in 2024 [3], addresses a fundamental limitation of both RT-2 and OpenVLA: their action tokenization approach is poorly suited to **dexterous manipulation** — tasks requiring smooth, continuous motion trajectories like folding laundry, assembling objects with precise tolerances, or wiping a surface.

Tokenized actions are inherently discrete: each action dimension is quantized into 256 bins, and the model produces one bin index per dimension. For tasks where sub-millimeter precision matters, or where the action trajectory must be smooth over time, this discretization introduces errors that compound into task failure.

π0 replaces the tokenized action head with a **flow matching** generative model, producing smooth, continuous action trajectories that are qualitatively superior to those produced by discretized prediction.

### 11.5.2 What Flow Matching Is

Flow matching [14] is a framework for learning to generate continuous samples by learning a vector field that transports samples from a simple prior distribution (typically Gaussian noise) to a target distribution (the distribution of valid robot actions).

Intuitively: to generate an action, start with a random noise vector and iteratively "flow" it toward a valid action by applying a learned vector field at each step. The vector field is parameterized by a neural network trained to predict the direction of flow given the current position in action space and the conditioning information (the visual-linguistic context from the VLA backbone).

Flow matching is related to **diffusion models** [15] but is computationally more efficient: it learns a direct, straight path from noise to signal, rather than the curved denoising trajectories learned by diffusion models. This makes inference faster — π0 can generate a full action trajectory in 10 denoising steps rather than the 100+ steps required by some diffusion policies.

For robot control, flow matching has two key advantages over autoregressive tokenized prediction:

1. **Temporal coherence:** The generated action trajectory is smooth across time steps, rather than a sequence of independently quantized decisions
2. **Fine-grained precision:** The output is a continuous vector, not a discretized bin index, enabling sub-millimeter accuracy when the training data supports it

### 11.5.3 π0 Architecture

π0 uses a **PaliGemma** VLM backbone [16] (a 3B parameter vision-language model from Google DeepMind) for the visual-linguistic understanding component, with the flow matching action expert as a parallel network that generates actions conditioned on the VLM's representations.

The two networks run in parallel during inference:
- The VLM processes the image and instruction and produces a conditioning representation
- The flow matching network takes the conditioning representation and iteratively refines a noise vector into an action trajectory

This **dual-network architecture** separates the semantic understanding task (handled by the large VLM backbone) from the motor generation task (handled by the lightweight flow matching expert), allowing each component to be optimized for its specific role.

### 11.5.4 Demonstrated Tasks

Physical Intelligence demonstrated π0 on a set of tasks specifically chosen to be difficult for prior VLA systems:

**Laundry folding.** Folding a towel or shirt requires smooth, spatially consistent motion over a large workspace, with contact forces that must be precisely controlled to avoid tangling the fabric. π0 achieved reliable towel folding across varied initial configurations.

**Table bussing.** Clearing a table — picking up dishes, glasses, and utensils of varied shapes and placing them in a bus tray — requires rapid context switching across different object types and grasp strategies. π0 performed this task with a bimanual robot, coordinating both arms.

**Box assembly.** Folding a flat cardboard blank into a box requires precise alignment of flaps and consistent force application. π0 completed box assembly reliably, a task that had previously required specialized jigs or human assistance.

These demonstrations were significant because they involved **bimanual dexterous manipulation** — two-arm coordination for tasks requiring precision and force control — which is far beyond the reach of single-arm tabletop systems like RT-2's evaluation environment.

### 11.5.5 Key Advantages Over RT-2 and OpenVLA

π0 represents a qualitative advance in three dimensions:

1. **Motion quality:** Flow matching produces smoother, more naturalistic trajectories than discretized token prediction
2. **Dexterous capability:** The continuous action representation enables fine manipulation that discrete bins cannot represent accurately
3. **Bimanual operation:** π0 is designed for and demonstrated on two-arm systems, whereas RT-2 and OpenVLA focus on single-arm manipulation

## 11.6 Action Tokenization Explained

### 11.6.1 Why Representing Actions as Tokens Works

The insight behind RT-2's action tokenization is that **any sequence prediction problem can be solved with a language model if the sequence elements can be represented as tokens**. Robot actions are sequences of numbers; numbers can be discretized into integer indices; integer indices can be vocabulary tokens. Therefore, robot control can be framed as next-token prediction.

This reframing has two major benefits:

**Training objective unification.** When actions are tokens, robot data and language data share the same training objective: cross-entropy loss on next-token prediction. The model can be trained on interleaved batches of language data (where the targets are word tokens) and robot data (where the targets are action tokens) without any architectural modification. The backpropagation gradient flows identically in both cases.

**Knowledge transfer.** The shared token representation creates a pathway for knowledge transfer from language pretraining to robot control. When the model's language representations already encode knowledge about objects, affordances, and spatial relationships, the fine-tuning on robot data can leverage that knowledge rather than building it from scratch.

### 11.6.2 Discretization of Continuous Action Spaces

The discretization process converts a continuous value `v ∈ [v_min, v_max]` into an integer bin index `b ∈ {0, 1, ..., B-1}` according to:

```python
# action_tokenization.py
# Discretization and de-tokenization of continuous robot actions

import numpy as np
from typing import NamedTuple


class ActionSpace(NamedTuple):
    """Defines the bounds and discretization of a single action dimension."""
    name: str
    min_val: float
    max_val: float
    num_bins: int = 256


# Standard 7-DOF action space for a Franka Panda arm
# Units: meters for translation, radians for rotation
FRANKA_ACTION_SPACE = [
    ActionSpace("delta_x",     min_val=-0.05, max_val=0.05,  num_bins=256),
    ActionSpace("delta_y",     min_val=-0.05, max_val=0.05,  num_bins=256),
    ActionSpace("delta_z",     min_val=-0.05, max_val=0.05,  num_bins=256),
    ActionSpace("delta_roll",  min_val=-0.25, max_val=0.25,  num_bins=256),
    ActionSpace("delta_pitch", min_val=-0.25, max_val=0.25,  num_bins=256),
    ActionSpace("delta_yaw",   min_val=-0.25, max_val=0.25,  num_bins=256),
    ActionSpace("gripper",     min_val=-1.0,  max_val=1.0,   num_bins=256),
]


def tokenize_action(
    action: np.ndarray,
    action_space: list[ActionSpace],
) -> list[int]:
    """
    Convert a continuous 7-DOF action vector into a list of integer bin indices.

    Args:
        action:        np.ndarray of shape (7,), continuous action values
        action_space:  List of ActionSpace definitions, one per dimension

    Returns:
        tokens: List of 7 integer bin indices in range [0, num_bins-1]
    """
    assert len(action) == len(action_space), (
        f"Action dim {len(action)} != action space dim {len(action_space)}"
    )

    tokens = []
    for value, space in zip(action, action_space):
        # Clip to valid range
        value = np.clip(value, space.min_val, space.max_val)

        # Linear discretization: map [min, max] -> [0, num_bins - 1]
        normalized = (value - space.min_val) / (space.max_val - space.min_val)
        bin_index = int(normalized * (space.num_bins - 1))
        bin_index = np.clip(bin_index, 0, space.num_bins - 1)
        tokens.append(bin_index)

    return tokens


def detokenize_action(
    tokens: list[int],
    action_space: list[ActionSpace],
) -> np.ndarray:
    """
    Convert a list of integer bin indices back to a continuous action vector.

    Args:
        tokens:        List of 7 integer bin indices
        action_space:  List of ActionSpace definitions

    Returns:
        action: np.ndarray of shape (7,), reconstructed continuous action
    """
    action = []
    for bin_index, space in zip(tokens, action_space):
        # Map bin index back to continuous value (center of bin)
        normalized = bin_index / (space.num_bins - 1)
        value = space.min_val + normalized * (space.max_val - space.min_val)
        action.append(value)

    return np.array(action, dtype=np.float32)


def compute_quantization_error(
    original: np.ndarray,
    action_space: list[ActionSpace],
) -> np.ndarray:
    """
    Compute the maximum quantization error (bin width / 2) for each dimension.
    """
    errors = []
    for space in action_space:
        bin_width = (space.max_val - space.min_val) / (space.num_bins - 1)
        errors.append(bin_width / 2.0)
    return np.array(errors)
```

### 11.6.3 Trade-Off: Resolution vs Vocabulary Size

The choice of bin count `B` is a critical design parameter governing the resolution-vocabulary-size trade-off:

| Bin Count (B) | Max Error (5 cm range) | Vocabulary Tokens Used | Notes |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---|
| 16 | ±1.56 mm | 16 | Too coarse for precise tasks |
| 64 | ±0.39 mm | 64 | Marginal precision |
| 256 | ±0.10 mm | 256 | RT-2 / OpenVLA standard |
| 1024 | ±0.024 mm | 1,024 | Near-continuous; larger vocab |
| Continuous | 0 | N/A | Flow matching / diffusion |

The standard choice of 256 bins represents a practical compromise: 0.1 mm resolution is sufficient for most tabletop manipulation tasks, and 256 tokens occupy less than 1% of a typical LLM vocabulary of 32,000 tokens.

### 11.6.4 Alternatives: Diffusion Policies and Flow Matching

The limitations of action tokenization — coarse discretization, independent per-dimension prediction ignoring action correlations, slow autoregressive decoding — motivated the development of alternative action representations:

**Diffusion Policy** [15] models the distribution over actions as a reverse diffusion process, iteratively denoising a Gaussian noise vector into a valid action. This produces multi-modal action distributions (the policy can learn to handle situations where multiple different actions are equally valid) and smooth trajectories, but requires many denoising iterations at inference time.

**Flow Matching** (π0) as described in Section 11.5.2 offers similar quality with fewer inference steps, representing the current state of the art for dexterous manipulation.

## 11.7 VLA Limitations and Open Challenges

### 11.7.1 Inference Latency

Inference latency is the most immediate practical constraint on VLA deployment. The table below summarizes measured latencies across the systems discussed:

| System | Parameters | Hardware | Inference Rate | Suitable for... |
|:---|:---:|:---|:---:|:---|
| RT-2-X (55B) | 55B | Google TPU v4 | ~1–3 Hz | Slow pick-and-place |
| RT-2-B (12B) | 12B | Google TPU v4 | ~10 Hz | Reactive tabletop tasks |
| OpenVLA-7B | 7B | NVIDIA A100 | ~6–8 Hz | Reactive tabletop tasks |
| π0 (3B backbone) | 3B + flow expert | NVIDIA A100 | ~10–15 Hz | Dexterous manipulation |
| Diffusion Policy | ~100M | NVIDIA RTX 3090 | ~10 Hz (50 steps) | Dexterous manipulation |

For comparison, biological motor control operates at approximately **100 Hz** for conscious voluntary movement, and human reflexes operate at effective frequencies well above 100 Hz. Even the fastest current VLA systems are an order of magnitude slower than the biological systems they aim to replicate.

### 11.7.2 Training Data Requirements

VLA systems are data-hungry. RT-2's training required 130,000+ robot demonstration episodes. OpenVLA used 970,000 episodes from Open X-Embodiment. Collecting this data requires physical robots, human teleoperators, and months of continuous operation.

Data collection bottlenecks create a fundamental asymmetry: tasks that are easy to demonstrate (pick-and-place on a flat table) are well-represented in training data; tasks that are difficult to demonstrate (bimanual dexterous assembly, whole-body locomotion) are underrepresented and consequently where VLA performance degrades most sharply.

Simulation-based data augmentation — generating robot demonstrations in synthetic environments and training on the mixed real-synthetic dataset — is an active area of research, but the sim-to-real gap for contact-rich dexterous manipulation remains a challenging problem.

### 11.7.3 Generalization Boundaries

Despite the emergent generalization demonstrated by RT-2, VLA systems have clear generalization boundaries. They fail systematically in several categories:

**Appearance shifts.** Objects seen in training with a particular texture, color, or background may not be recognized when those properties change. A model trained on a white countertop may fail when the same task is performed on a dark countertop.

**Semantic ambiguity.** Natural language is ambiguous, and VLA models inherit this ambiguity. "Put it there" requires resolving two references ("it" and "there") that may have multiple valid interpretations depending on scene context that the model may not correctly ground.

**Long-horizon tasks.** VLA models predict a single action or short action sequence at each inference step. Tasks requiring dozens of steps (making a sandwich, assembling furniture) require long-horizon planning mechanisms that current VLA systems do not provide natively.

### 11.7.4 Safety Concerns and Unpredictable Failure Modes

The opacity of large neural networks creates safety challenges that are qualitatively different from those of classical robot controllers. A classical trajectory planner will fail gracefully and predictably when given an infeasible goal; a VLA model may produce a physically dangerous action in response to a slightly out-of-distribution input.

Failure modes observed in deployed VLA systems include:

- **Confident misdirection:** The model predicts a plausible-looking action that moves the robot toward an incorrect object, with no uncertainty signal
- **Goal obsession:** The model continues attempting a task even after it has clearly failed, compounding the failure
- **Instruction hallucination:** The model produces actions consistent with an instruction it "hallucinated" rather than the instruction given

These failure modes are particularly concerning in environments where the robot operates near humans, where a failed grasp could result in dropped objects, where a missed navigation step could result in collision. Current VLA systems lack the uncertainty quantification and safety monitoring mechanisms needed for deployment in high-stakes environments.

## 11.9 Chapter Summary

The Vision-Language-Action architecture represents the current frontier of robot intelligence: a single neural network that fuses perception, natural language understanding, and motor control. By jointly encoding visual and language tokens through transformer attention, VLA models can follow instructions about objects and situations never individually represented in their training data — a qualitatively different capability from classical task programming, and one that makes the humanoid form factor practically useful.

RT-2's central contribution was demonstrating that emergent generalization arises from scale and co-fine-tuning: a large VLM trained simultaneously on web data and robot demonstrations learns to connect language concepts to physical actions through distributed statistical learning rather than explicit symbolic mapping. OpenVLA reproduced this result at 7B parameters using open-source components and the Open X-Embodiment dataset, making the approach accessible beyond industrial research labs. π0 identified the structural limitation of action tokenization — discretized bins cannot represent the smooth, high-frequency trajectories that dexterous manipulation requires — and replaced them with flow matching to enable the continuous control that bimanual assembly tasks demand.

The fundamental limitations of current VLA systems are structural, not incidental: the data volumes required for generalizable policies are enormous, inference latency constrains deployment to tasks that tolerate second-scale response times, and failure modes are opaque in ways that create genuine safety concerns for human-proximate environments. These constraints define the research agenda, not a ceiling on the approach. Chapter 12 builds directly on the VLA substrate described here — adding a speech interface so that a human can direct the robot's capabilities by speaking, rather than requiring a structured programming interface.
